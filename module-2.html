Think about this: LLMs are trained on text, right? And the context that drives attention, that's all just text too. It's all just words for AI, patterns in text—that's the air it breathes, the water it swims in. It doesn't live in our world; it lives in a world of text.

Given what we've already learned about training and context, this shouldn't really be surprising. Remember: these things are—at least in some sense—autocomplete on steroids. And when push came to shove, the AI in the video did the same thing autocomplete does. From the text it's trained on, it's learned to associate running a mile with taking about 10 minutes. The likely continuation of "I ran a mile in about ____" is "10 minutes", in just the same way that the likely continuation of "Okay I'll be there in about _____" is "5 minutes".

In other words, when push came to shove, it did the same thing autocomplete does. The likely continuation of "I ran a mile in about" is "10 minutes", in just the same way that the likely continuation of "I'll be there in about" is "5 minutes". It didn't check its answer against a stop clock. It checked its answer against the geometry of its training.

Now that's a problem. Because the thing is, these models sound like they know what they're doing. They sound smart. Oftentimes, they sound like authorities.

And in normal human conversations, when someone speaks confidently and fluently about a subject, we take that as a cue that they know what they're talking about. If someone can fluently say plausible medical jargon, we think there's a good chance they're a qualified medical professional. That's usually reasonable: in normal human conversations, fluency and confidence are reliable signals of competence.

But that connection isn't always there with AI. Since they've been trained on such a huge database of our writing, confidence and fluency comes easily--the text is practially drenched in it. They can easily produce the voice of authority even when they haven't earned it. The signal is no longer reliable.

So what does that means for us? Is there a way to use these things without getting fooled?

Video

Okay, so three habits: don't lead the witness, proportion your trust, and check. They're all three worth remembering, and maybe you want to write them down. But I'll be realistic with myself--you probably don't want to write them down.

Stay in the driver's seat. Don't let the AI take the wheel.

So really, there's just one habit with three different faces, and that habit is: stay in the driver's seat. Don't let the AI take the wheel. Refuse to outsource your judgment. You decide what to ask and how to ask it. You decide how much you should trust, and you decide what's worth checking.

And I should also point out here that you decide what to put in, in the first place. The main point of this lesson is to underscore that the output of AI can't be trusted. But you should also keep in mind that the companies that run AI also can't be trusted. So: don't feed these things your personal information. No names, addresses, social security numbers, medical histories. Everything you input can be stored and fed into the training for the next iteration of the model. So be as careful about what you put in as you are about what you take out.
