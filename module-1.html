Module One

How AI Works

Artificial intelligence is changing everything — how we access information, how we solve problems, and how we tell our stories. Large language models like ChatGPT are not just software. They are a new kind of collaborator, one that learns from the full breadth of human knowledge and puts that knowledge at your fingertips. Used wisely, these tools don't replace your thinking. They extend it. Understanding how they work is the first step toward using them well.

In these modules, we're going to talk about AI—about how it works, where it goes wrong, and what Daemen expects of you when and if you use AI in your coursework. I'm Dr. Mark Warren, Associate Professor of Philosophy and the Chair of Daemen's Philosophy and Religious Studies department. Welcome to your crash course in AI at Daemen.

But before we do any of that, let's take a step back and ask a fundamental question: Just how is it that people trained a hunk of silicon to talk like a person?

The short answer is that AI that writes text like this—what we call "Large Language Models"—work like auto-complete on steroids. That's a really short answer, though; it misses out on a lot. But it's the basic idea: you feed the machine some text, and it draws on an enormous library of data to give you a likely continuation.

I'm giving you the simplest version of this story I can. It's like when you first learn about atoms in school: they show you electrons orbiting around the nucleus like planets around a star. And we've known for a very long time that's not really correct—atoms aren't like that. But it's usefully wrong. It helps you learn the basic idea.

With those two ingredients—training and context—Large Language Models can do a lot. The training gives us a multi-dimensional map, a geometry that keeps track of word meanings across a corpus of text that spans most of the Internet. And self-attention gives us the context that lets the LLM navigate across that map.

The result is a computer that can carry on a conversation, that seems to understand what you're saying, that can draw on its enormous training corpus to explain new ideas, offer advice, and even solve famous mathematical puzzles that have evaded the world's best mathematicians for decades.

It does things that make it seem like it understands. But does it really, or is that just an illusion?
